Overarching
CX learnings

1Customer access is not structurally managed

AutoStore lacks a consistent system for accessing end-customers. Contact depends on personal networks, no tool tracks "total pressure" on customers, and internal gatekeeping is high.

Result: securing just 5–10 interviews has taken disproportionate effort — a symptom of a structural gap, not a resourcing issue. This makes any customer-facing initiative (not just CX) harder and riskier than it needs to be.

2Limited understanding of partner commercial models

AutoStore has inconsistent visibility into how partners' commercial models shape behavior toward end-customers. Many partner SLAs exclude software upgrades and proactive optimization.

Customers end up running 2+ year-old software, missing significant innovation. In some models, software upgrades represent a financial liability due to integration risk. Partners sell "build-it-and-don't-touch-it" offerings where any post-go-live work becomes a commercial dispute. AutoStore has no consistent way of knowing what partners have sold — or are incentivized to deliver.

3Service quality & innovation blocked at the partner layer

Because partners are not incentivized for continuous improvement, customers often never receive performance enhancements embedded in newer software.

This extends beyond software to general customer support and optimization, which are often outside SLAs and therefore margin-dilutive for partners. While some partners actively invest in managed services and proactive optimization, the result overall is high variability in service quality and customer experience, even when AutoStore fulfills its own responsibilities.

How I suggest
we move
forward

01

Use the CX Immersion artifact as the North Star

Complete the baseline: current-state narrative, CX variability map, CX truths, blind spots, and value-at-risk framing. Ground all strategic decisions in reality.

The artifact will include: A clear narrative of what it feels like to be an AutoStore customer today · A CX variability map across customers, partners, and journey stages · 5–7 CX truths (core, slightly uncomfortable insights from synthesis) · Explicit blind spots around ownership, partner assumptions, and internal beliefs vs. customer reality · Synthesized moments, frictions, value impacts, and quotes captured with strict data-discipline · A value-at-risk framing for executive alignment · A bridge into Phase 2 partner diagnostics. In short: the factual, organization-wide baseline we need before designing our CX vision, strategy, and partner model.
02

Re-evaluate Partner–AutoStore dynamics

Structural misalignments in partner incentives, SLAs, and responsibilities are a major systemic CX opportunity. Phase 2 should define clear responsibilities, shared KPIs, alignment on innovation delivery, and transparent service offerings.

03

Define pragmatic Voice of Customer approach

After Forrester input, propose minimum viable end-customer measurement — what, when, who owns — including partner-aligned metric sharing if viable.
